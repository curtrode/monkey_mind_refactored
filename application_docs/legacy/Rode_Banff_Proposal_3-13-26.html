Monkey Mind — Project Proposal (Curt Rode)

Inception

I first encountered Nick Montfort's work on Memory Slam while searching for teachable examples of electronic literature for my Digital Creative Writing course several years ago. Compared to works like "Taroko Gorge," the stochastic text engines collected in MemSlam were more accessible, easier for students to read, modify, and adapt. As a newer practitioner of computational writing myself, I found the "simplicity" of the engines running "Love Letters" (Strachey) and "House of Dust" (Knowles & Tenney) encouraging and conceptually clarifying. Originally, "Monkey Mind" was an apprentice's remix, not something I planned to propose for a residency on Computational Writing, but coming back to it recently and learning more about the writing faculty at Banff, I'm confident there is much more to develop.

Evolution

Where "House of Dust" and "Love Letters" exist as more traditional poetic monologues, "Monkey Mind" aspired in its earlier stages to create a cluttered dialogue that dramatizes the anxieties of intimacy. It presents two voices generating text simultaneously, each assembling phrases from relational subjects ("you are my," "I am not your," "we were our") and abstract ("silence," "dream," "darkness," "freedom") or pastoral ("horses," "meadow," "yellow bird") predicates.

The piece now uses a terminal-style interface with text-to-speech narration (both synthetic machine and AI-generated voices). It is also more interactive. The two voices begin muted, each set to a default rate; the viewer can choose to activate one or both voices, which TTS technology to use, and how quickly each voice processes its anxieties.

The title refers to the Buddhist concept of "monkey mind," restless consciousness swinging from thought to thought. The subtitle, Too many tabs open, updates the metaphor.

Proposed Development

The central move in the next phase of "Monkey Mind" is a transition from stochastic generation to large-language-model improvisation. The piece will stage this transition as a dramatic event: two voices trapped in scripted patterns—unable to truly connect—until one begins to actually respond. The formal shift from mechanical to improvisational IS the emotional arc. Think of a jazz standard: the stochastic engine plays the head, the composed melody; the LLM picks up the horn and solos over the changes.

A working prototype of this transition already exists as a single-voice monologue. The stochastic engine runs for approximately twenty lines, accumulating its output as context. That context is then fed to an LLM, which takes over generation in three stages: first echoing the original grammar, then bending it—introducing new words, longer fragments, something like intention—and finally drifting into free improvisation, finding language for things the stochastic engine could not say. The voice itself shifts: a synthetic machine voice during the stochastic phase gives way to an AI-generated voice as the language begins to improvise, making the transition audible.

At the residency, I plan to expand this into a multi-model dialogue. Each voice would be driven by a different LLM—each with genuinely different training, different tendencies, different ways of interpreting the same emotional territory. The author's role becomes that of a director, giving each model-voice psychological instructions through system prompts that play with or against its natural grain. Two people in a relationship aren't running the same software; different models make that literal.

Asymmetric transition: One voice begins improvising while the other remains stochastic—dramatizing the intimacy dynamic of one person reaching while the other is still scripted.

TTS embodiment: The voices gain distinct AI-generated vocalizations, spatialized in stereo, adding a dimension of physical presence to the text.

Why This Residency

"Monkey Mind" was inspired by and literally built on Montfort's MemSlam and lives within the computational writing tradition this residency represents. Kalen Iwamoto's work provides a fascinating example of how "Monkey Mind" might be developed into other technologies, both digital and analog. And while Travesty Generator explores other thematic concerns, it would be a tremendous pleasure to learn from Lillian-Yvonne Bertram and how she coded her syntactical structures.

Montfort's work asks what language does when rules assemble it. "Monkey Mind" asks what language does when different minds with different fears improvise with it. With a dedicated block of time in an engaged community, I have the tools to find out. The insights from this residency will also directly inform my first offering of a Creative Coding course in Spring 2027.

Related work: Branch et al., "Improbotics: Exploring the Imitation Game using Commercial LLM Chatbots" (2024); Zhang & Eger, "Multi-Agent Poetry Generation" (2024); Johnston, ReRites (2017–2019).
