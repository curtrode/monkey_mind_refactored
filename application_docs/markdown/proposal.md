"Monkey Mind" is an electronic literature piece that stages a transition from rule-based stochastic text generation to large-language-model improvisation, and makes that transition the dramatic arc of the work.

In its current form, two voices generate text simultaneously in a terminal-style interface, each assembling phrases from relational subjects ("you are my," "I am not your," "we were our") and abstract ("silence," "dream," "darkness," "freedom") or pastoral ("horses," "meadow," "yellow bird") predicates. The voices run at different speeds, producing an overlapping texture of possession and negation. Text-to-speech narration, both synthetic machine and AI-generated voices, transforms the reading into a listening experience.

## The Transition

The central formal innovation is what happens next. Think of a jazz standard: the stochastic engine plays the head, the composed melody; the LLM picks up the horn and solos over the changes. The handoff unfolds in three phases, echo/bend/drift, moving from strict reuse of the source grammar to freer improvisation.

The voice itself shifts with the language. During the stochastic phase, a synthetic machine voice speaks the lines. As the LLM takes over, an AI-generated voice replaces it. The transition is not just visible on screen but also audible: mechanical speech giving way to something that sounds like a person trying to reach another person. A working prototype of this single-voice monologue is included in the portfolio, with a video recording for reviewers without an API key: <https://youtu.be/-DCPQpnhN_s>.

## Proposed Residency Work

What exists now is a two-voice stochastic duet and a single-voice LLM improvisation prototype. At Banff, I will build the next stage: a multi-model dialogue in which each voice is driven by a different LLM with distinct tendencies and interpretive habits. My role is directorial: I design and revise system prompts, transition logic (echo/bend/drift), and timing so model differences produce legible relational dynamics rather than noise. The current prototype uses a fixed "him/her" pairing; this version will support broader relationship configurations beyond that starting point. During the September 7-18, 2026 residency, I will build and test the multi-model system in week one, then refine it in week two into a presentation-ready demonstration excerpt and a documented prompt framework for post-residency development. I author the lexicon, constraints, prompt dramaturgy, and final curation; the models generate candidate lines within that designed frame.

## Lineage

"Monkey Mind" began as an apprentice's remix of Montfort's MemSlam. I first encountered those stochastic text engines while searching for teachable examples of electronic literature for my Digital Creative Writing course and found their simplicity both encouraging and conceptually clarifying. The project has since outgrown its origins, but it remains in conversation with the computational writing tradition this residency represents. Iwamoto's work suggests how it might extend into other technologies; Bertram's syntactical structures in *Travesty Generator* offer a model for how coded grammar can carry thematic weight. Montfort's work asks what language does when rules assemble it. "Monkey Mind" asks what language does when different minds with different fears improvise with it. The insights from this residency will directly inform my first offering of a Creative Coding course in Spring 2027.
