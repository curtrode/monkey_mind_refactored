Banff Centre for Arts and Creativity

Dear Adjudication Committee,

I returned to "Monkey Mind" after several years away, almost by accident — and then saw this call shared by Mark Marino through the Electronic Literature Organization mailing list. That coincidence clarified the next step for a project I had not quite finished thinking through.

"Monkey Mind" is an electronic literature piece that stages a transition from rule-based stochastic text generation to LLM improvisation, making that transition itself the dramatic arc. What exists now is a two-voice stochastic duet and a single-voice improvisation prototype. Two voices assemble phrases from relational subjects and abstract or pastoral predicates, running at different speeds to produce an overlapping texture of possession and negation. The LLM picks up those patterns and solos over them — echo, bend, drift — while the voice shifts from synthetic machine speech to something that sounds like a person trying to reach another person. The piece is not autobiographical, but it dramatizes patterns of relational anxiety and overthinking I have experienced and observed in others. That tension is the material, not the memoir.

The question I keep returning to is both technical and poetic: can prompt design introduce what I call lyric suspicion — language that begins to question its own assumptions rather than simply expressing them? I author the lexicon, constraints, prompt dramaturgy, and final curation; the models generate candidate lines within that frame. One thing I know is unresolved: the drift phase can settle into sentiment. I need strategies that preserve friction and surprise, and I need practitioners who understand the problem to help me find them.

During the September 7–18, 2026 residency, I will build a multi-model dialogue in which each voice is driven by a different LLM with distinct interpretive tendencies — build and test in week one, refine into a presentation-ready excerpt and documented prompt framework in week two. The project began as an apprentice's remix of Montfort's *MemSlam*, and his influence remains central. I want to test this work against that influence and broaden it through Banff's faculty, workshop structure, and peer critique. As a poet directing TCU's Digital Culture & Data Analytics program, I have productive constraints but not the depth of e-lit feedback this piece now requires. I want to offer peers a practitioner's perspective on pedagogy and production workflow, and to bring curiosity rather than certainty.

The results will directly inform a new Creative Coding course in Spring 2027, and support publication, performance, and installation possibilities beyond Banff.

Sincerely,  
Curt Rode
