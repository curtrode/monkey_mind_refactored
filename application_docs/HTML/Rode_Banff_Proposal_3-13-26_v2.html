Monkey Mind — Project Proposal (Curt Rode)

The Work

"Monkey Mind" is an electronic literature piece that stages a transition from rule-based stochastic text generation to large-language-model improvisation—and makes that transition the dramatic arc of the work.

In its current form, two voices generate text simultaneously in a terminal-style interface, each assembling phrases from relational subjects ("you are my," "I am not your," "we were our") and abstract ("silence," "dream," "darkness," "freedom") or pastoral ("horses," "meadow," "yellow bird") predicates. The voices run at different speeds, producing an overlapping texture of possession and negation. Text-to-speech narration—both synthetic machine and AI-generated voices, spatialized in stereo—transforms the reading into a listening experience. The title refers to the Buddhist concept of restless consciousness; the subtitle, Too many tabs open, updates the metaphor.

The Transition

The central formal innovation is what happens next. Think of a jazz standard: the stochastic engine plays the head, the composed melody; the LLM picks up the horn and solos over the changes. The piece stages this handoff in three phases. First the LLM echoes the original grammar—same word pools, same two-clause structure. Then it bends the patterns: new words enter, fragments lengthen, something like intention emerges. Finally it drifts into free improvisation, finding language for things the stochastic engine could not say.

The voice itself shifts with the language. During the stochastic phase, a synthetic machine voice speaks the lines. As the LLM takes over, an AI-generated voice replaces it. The transition is not just visible on screen but audible—mechanical speech giving way to something that sounds like a person trying to reach someone. A working prototype of this single-voice monologue is included in the portfolio (with a video recording for reviewers without an API key).

Proposed Residency Work

At Banff, I plan to expand the monologue into a multi-model dialogue. Each voice would be driven by a different LLM—each with genuinely different training, different tendencies, different ways of interpreting the same emotional territory. The author's role becomes that of a director, giving each model-voice psychological instructions through system prompts that play with or against its natural grain. Two people in a relationship aren't running the same software; different models make that literal.

Lineage

I first encountered Nick Montfort's work on Memory Slam while searching for teachable examples of electronic literature for my Digital Creative Writing course. The stochastic text engines collected in MemSlam—running "Love Letters" (Strachey) and "House of Dust" (Knowles & Tenney)—were accessible enough for students to read, modify, and adapt, and conceptually clarifying for me as a practitioner. Originally, "Monkey Mind" was an apprentice's remix, not something I planned to propose for a residency on Computational Writing, but coming back to it recently and learning more about the writing faculty at Banff, I'm confident there is much more to develop.

"Monkey Mind" was inspired by and literally built on Montfort's MemSlam and lives within the computational writing tradition this residency represents. Kalen Iwamoto's work provides a fascinating example of how "Monkey Mind" might be developed into other technologies, both digital and analog. And while Travesty Generator explores other thematic concerns, it would be a tremendous pleasure to learn from Lillian-Yvonne Bertram and how she coded her syntactical structures.

Montfort's work asks what language does when rules assemble it. "Monkey Mind" asks what language does when different minds with different fears improvise with it. The insights from this residency will directly inform my first offering of a Creative Coding course in Spring 2027.

Related work: Branch et al., "Improbotics: Exploring the Imitation Game using Commercial LLM Chatbots" (2024); Zhang & Eger, "Multi-Agent Poetry Generation" (2024); Johnston, ReRites (2017–2019).
